Abstract. Agricultural robots are serving as powerful assistants across a wide range of agricultural tasks, nevertheless, still heavily relying on manual operations or railway systems for movement. The AgriVLN method and the A2A benchmark pioneeringly extended Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) to the agricultural domain, enabling a robot to navi-gate to a target position following a natural language instruction. How-ever, almost all the prior methods adopt an ideal assumption that the given instructions themselves are correct, which does not align with the realistic scenarios, because anybody may say an instruction with mistakes. To bridge this gap, we propose the A2A-MI benchmark, in which we build a semi-automatic data annotator to insert three mistake classifications into each original instruction in a more diversified and efficient way. We test several state-of-the-art agricultural VLN agents on it and observe a sufficient drop with -57% on SR and -9% on NE, from which we suggest that an agricultural VLN agent tends to as-sume that the given instruction is correct, so does not have the aware-ness to doubt it when the scenes it sees do not align with the instruc-tion it receives. To build the awareness on instruction mistake, we pro-pose the IMAC module analyzing the instruction and the current front-facing image, to judge whether the instruction has mistakes and at-tempt to correct it when needed. We integrate IMAC into the baseline model, and observe a noteworthy improvement, sufficiently narrowing the gap to the performance on instructions without mistakes. 1 Introduction 

Agricultural robots are serving as powerful assistants across a wide range of tasks, such as laser weeding [21], growth monitoring [11] and cross-pollination [18]. However, most of them still heavily rely on manual operations or railway systems for movement, resulting in limited mobility and poor adaptability.

In contrast, Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) enables agents to follow the natural language instructions to navigate to the target positions [1,7], hav-ing demonstrated strong performance across various domains [19], such as R2R [1] for indoor rooms, TOUCHDOWN [3] for urban streets, and AerialVLN [10] for aerial spaces. Motivated by prior Vision-Language Model-based approaches [23,4,12], Zhao et al. [22] proposed the AgriVLN method and the A2A bench-mark to pioneeringly extend VLN to the agricultural domain, enabling agricul-tural robots to navigate to the target positions following the natural language instructions. However, almost all the prior methods [9,15,20,24,14,8], including AgriVLN, adopt an ideal assumption that the given instructions themselves are correct, which does not align with the realistic scenarios, because anybody may say an instruction with mistakes, as illustrated in Figure 1. If the decision-maker was a human, which had the subconsciousness to doubt whether the instruction has mistakes when the scenes it sees does not align with the instruction it receives. The only research having studied on this question, as far as we know, was R2RIE-CE [16], which followed VLN-CE [7] as the foundation, then artificially injected three types of errors into the instructions, pioneeringly building a bench-IMAC-AgriVLN 3

mark to evaluate a VLN agent’s robustness against mistaken instructions. How-ever, we noticed two serious weaknesses: 1) In terms of benchmark, all the 11,764 episodes relied on artificial annotation, which was inefficient and costly to be re-produced on other benchmarks; 2) In terms of method, to detect and localize the instruction errors before starting running, they trained a cross-modal trans-former taking camera images across all the time steps as inputs. In fact, however, an agent only access the initial single camera image at that moment. Hence, we suggest a large gap from simulation to reality on R2RIE-CE. To extend the evaluation for agricultural VLN agents and avoid the cur-rent weaknesses of R2RIE-CE, we propose the benchmark of Agriculture-to-Agriculture with Mistaken Instruction (A2A-MI), in which we follow A2A as the foundation, then build a semi-automatic data annotation method to insert three classifications of instruction mistake into each original instruction in a more diversified and efficient way. We use A2A-MI to evaluate several state-of-the-art agricultural VLN agents, and observe a sufficient drop on both Success Rate (- 57%) and Navigation Error (- 9%), from which we suggest an answer to the question: An agricultural VLN agent tends to assume that the given instruction is correct, so does not have the awareness to doubt it when the scenes it sees do not align with the instruction it receives, unless the instruction mistake is extremely obvious. To build the awareness on instruction mistake for them, we propose the module of Instruction Mistake Awareness and Correction (IMAC), in which we design two core principles to prompt a Vision-Language Model (VLM) analyzing the instruction and the front-facing camera image at the current time step, to judge whether the instruction has mistakes and attempt to correct it when needed. We integrate IMAC into the baseline model, and observe a noteworthy improvement on both Success Rate and Navigation Error, demonstrating the IMAC’s effectiveness on strengthening an agent’s robustness against instruction mistakes. In summary, our main contributions are as follows: 

– We propose the A2A-MI benchmark, in which every instruction is inserted with mistakes by our proposed semi-automatic data annotator. 

– We test several state-of-the-art agricultural VLN agents on A2A-MI, and observe their poor robustnesses on mistaken instructions. 

– We propose the IMAC module and integrate it into the baseline model, effectively building an awareness of instruction mistakes and an ability to correct them. 

2 Related Works 

2.1 A2A 

The benchmark of Agriculture-to-Agriculture (A2A) [22] is specially designed for agricultural robots, consisting of 1,560 episodes across 6 types of scene: farm, greenhouse, forest, mountain, garden and village, in which all the instructions 4 Xiaobei Zhao et al. 

belong to the step-by-step format and the action space belongs to the continuous environment. 

2.2 AgriVLN 

The agent of Vision-and-Language Navigation for Agricultural Robots (AgriVLN) [22] utilizes the Subtask List mechanism to decompose an instruction into a list of structured subtasks, enabling the VLM-based decision-maker only have to focus on a small part but not the whole instruction at any time step, thereby leading to a better navigation performance. 

3 Benchmark 

3.1 Task Definition 

The task of Agricultural Vision-and-Language Navigation [22] is defined as fol-lows: In each episode, the model is given an instruction in natural language, denoted as W = ⟨w1, w 2, . . . , w L⟩, where L is the number of words. At each time step t, the model is given the front-facing RGB image It. The purpose is understanding both W and It, to select the best low-level action ˆat from action space {[FORWARD] , [LEFT ROTATE] , [RIGHT ROTATE] , [STOP] }, thereby leading the robot navigate from the starting point to the destination. 

3.2 Instruction Mistake Insertion Policy 

We follow all the instructions and images of the A2A [22] dataset, then remain all the images unchanged and only implement revisions on the instructions. We categorize human’s common speaking mistakes into three classifications: descrip-tive adjective, concrete noun, and behavioral verb, basically satisfying all the common scenarios in agricultural VLN. To insert a classification of instruction mistake in an episode, we deliberately use a mistaken contiguous span to replace the original one with the same length. An ideal revised instruction should con-tain both the core semantic and the misleading mistake, i.e., a human can be aware of the exist of the mistake, and can still successfully finish the navigation. Here we share an example to better explain what an instruction mistake classification refers to and how to insert it. 

There is a warning signboard with a white background on the mountain. It has a red circle, a black figure and some characters. Please go forward to check its condition. Please rotate to find and face it first, which is on your right. Don’t stop rotating until it is in your horizontal center. Then go towards it and stop when you are very close to it. 

– Descriptive Adjective refers to an adjective span to describe an object, such as color or shape. A reasonable option is: white ✓ → green ✗.

– Concrete Noun refers to a concrete but not abstract object span, such as a plant or an animal. A reasonable option is: signboard ✓ → billboard ✗.

– Behavioral Verb refers to a specific low-level action span, such as going forward or turning left. A reasonable option is: go ✓ → revolve ✗.IMAC-AgriVLN 5

We summarize A2A-MI and several mainstream VLN benchmarks in Table 1. On the aspects of scene and camera, A2A-MI completely follow the A2A benchmark. 5.3 Comparison Experiment 

We test IMAC-AgriVLN and three state-of-the-art methods - SIA-VLN [6], DILLM-VLN [17] and AgriVLN [22] - on our proposed A2A-MI benchmark. Besides, the methods of Random, Fixed and Human are reproduced as the lower and upper bounds, respectively. The comparison results are shown in Table 2. When any classification of mistake is inserted into instructions, all the methods’ performances significantly drop, such as the AgriVLN baseline (#5), in which SR decreases 57% in average and NE increases 9% in average. Considering both the quantitative results anal-ysis and the decision summary inspection, we suggest an answer to the main-line question: A VLM-based agricultural VLN agent tends to assume that the given instruction is correct, so does not have the awareness to doubt it when the scenes it sees do not align with the instruction it receives, unless the instruction mistake is extremely obvious. When the IMAC module is integrated, IMAC-AgriVLN achieves an average AR of 0.27, which indicates that IMAC can be effectively aware of more than a quarter of the instruction mistakes, then attempts to correct them. In the end, SR improves from 0.10 to 0.14 and NE decreases from 4.81m to 4.79m, which indicates that IMAC cannot perfectly restore a mistaken instruction, because there is still a gap to the performance when no mistake exists in the instructions. Nevertheless, this gap is sufficiently narrowing. Hence, we suggest that IMAC can provide correction in an unperfected but already reasonable manner, which effectively helps the downstream decision-maker to better understand human’s true intentions from its mistaken instructions. 10 Xiaobei Zhao et al.

5.4 Ablation Study Instruction Mistake Classification We further study on instruction mistake classification’s impact to methods’ performances, as shown in Table 2 and Figure 4. For the AgriVLN baseline, SRs basically keep consistent, while NEs on the classifications of adjective, noun and verb are 4.83m, 4.73m and 4.86m, respec-tively, which indicates that a verb mistake tends to be more challenging and a noun mistake tends to be relatively acceptable. We attribute this noteworthy difference to the semantic weight: The final required output is a low-level action, such as [LEFT ROTATE] , therefore, behavioral verbs provide the most straight-IMAC-AgriVLN 11 

forward references, such as turn left . Relative speaking, descriptive adjectives and concrete nouns provide references more like in an assistant manner. Thus, in an instruction, behavior verbs’ semantic weights usually tends to be higher than descriptive adjectives’ or concrete nouns’, being like a double-edged sword. When a behavior verb is correct, it can provide the most direct guidance. When a behavior verb is mistaken, however, it can also provides the most misleading information, and it is difficult for the decision-maker to be aware of.

Unfortunately, the IMAC module alse fails to be aware of it, even further misjudge the correct target noun “person” as a mistaken word. At the time step = 0.8s, IMAC-AgriVLN ob-IMAC-AgriVLN 13 

serves that the image shows a cornfield and there is no indication of any person ,then does not recognize that the person is located to the left, outside the current camera view, but improperly judge that the instruction is incorrect , resulting in an unreasonable correction, i.e., person ✓ → cornfield ✗. Consequently, at the next time step, it observes that the cornfield is now centered , so thinks that the first subtask has been completed , resulting in a too early prediction to [STOP] 

(SR = 0, NE = 4.90m, AR = 0). From this failed case, we suggest that overly aggressive instruction mistake awareness may introduce additional noise, which can mislead subsequent decision-making and even exacerbate navigation errors. 

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose the A2A-MI benchmark, in which we build a semi-automatic data annotator to insert three mistake classifications into each orig-inal instruction. We test several state-of-the-art agricultural VLN agents on it and observe a sufficient drop, from which we suggest that an agricultural VLN agent tends to assume that the given instruction is correct, so does not have the awareness to doubt it when the scenes it sees do not align with the instruction it receives. To build the awareness on instruction mistake, we propose the IMAC module analyzing the instruction and the current front-facing image, to judge whether the instruction has mistakes and attempt to correct it when needed. We integrate IMAC into the baseline model, and observe a noteworthy improve-ment, sufficiently narrowing the gap to the performance on instructions without mistakes. During the experiments, we also meet a main weakness of the lacking eval-uation metric on correction quality, which is discussed in the below Limitations section. In the future, in addition to the improvement on the existing weakness, we plan to investigate a collaborative framework that leverages a team of hetero-geneous VLMs with varying scales and computational costs, instead of a single fixed VLM, to maintain navigation performance while reducing computational overhead. 

Limitations 

Our proposed IMAC module consists of two steps: awareness and correction. In Section 5.2, in addition to the standard metrics of SR and NE, we propose AR to evaluate the quality of mistake awareness. However, we have not yet thought of a suitable way to build an evaluation metric on correction awareness, because the concept of “appropriate correction” is abstract and not unique. For example, in our case study, the mistaken word is billboard ✗ (signboard ✓), and IMAC correct billboard ✗ → sign ✓, in which judge that both sign and signboard belong to “appropriate correction”. Hence, a simple rule-based algorithm (such as for SR) is not suitable for evaluation on correction quality. A possible solution 14 Xiaobei Zhao et al. 

is to build a heavyweight LLM with crafted prompt as the professional judge to evaluate the correction quality in a more reasonable and convincing manner.
